Ukraine Invasion: A Sad Reminder

The invasion of Ukraine is an unwelcome reminder that my own father, Albert, was subjected to similar ordeals imposed by a Russian dictatorship. His family were targeted by Stalin's repressions.

The turmoil started with his grandparents being dispossessed of all they owned. For a child that was difficult to comprehend. However, nothing could prepare him or his family for the programme of genocide openly planned by Soviet leaders.

My Father's Story

c.1930 Niiskovitsa, Ingria, Russia

In blue stripes my father Albert Georgijev poses next to his mother Aliide. She has good reason to look anxious: Stalin's propaganda machine has begun to demonise an entire demographic encouraging a hatred of so-called kulaks which as historian Orlando Figes points out, the inclusion of this family depended on the specific politruk (political official) responsible for compiling the lists of 'enemies of the people.'

L to R: Ludmilla, Rosalie, Salme, Aliide, Niina and Albert.

Madis and Maarja Noole Aliide's parents, (front left) were withour doubt successful farmers in Markuse: they provided regular employment and sold produce in Gatchina and St Petersburg. For these 'crimes' they were predictably targeted by politruks. Condemned as 'Enemies of the State' (Kulaks) all they owned was confiscated and they were sentenced to be deported. However, this was never expedited!

Aliide was brought up in her parents large farm house, Markuse. She was a skilled seamstress. She had trained in Narva, Estonia, stayed with relatives and left vowing to return. Aliide was the proud owner of a sewing machine which she used throughout her life to provide a service of tailoring and repairing cloth. Albert could recall the machine being used all day and how they as children were all taught to sew.

Vassili Georgijev was an orphan raised by a widow in Niiskovitsa, Ingria. Details of his origin are scant and the Bulgarian surname a mystery. The wedding photograph mainly consists of Aliide's family.

Madis and Maarja were not exiled to the East. When local agents came to collect them they could not be located and missed the train. For reasons unknown, the authorities isolated them in nearby Volosova. Salme (Albert's younger sister) recalled two cats, Ruti and Ints, and a dog, Pontu, being left at Markuse. Despite warnings Madis and Maarja made clandestine visits to their children. Source: image of eviction. Yury Dmitriev.

After years of building up his farm Vassili lost everything to the Collective agency. Its officials put him to work as a labourer on his own land, permitted him a solitary cow, and paid him in grain which he had to take to the mill to be ground. The humiliation was complete. Sadly, most Collectives were mismanaged and despite political slogans to the contrary there was a shortage of food throughout the country.

Additional impositions on the family made life extremely difficult for Aliide. Part of the family home was reappropriated as a shop which sold farm produce and vodka. Public access to the house compromised the safety of Aliide and her daughters.
Yet another worry was Vassili: enforced into a humbling submission his despair lifted by vodka.

Furthermore, there was an attempt to snatch Aliide's sewing machine. An altercation ensued with Aliide and her children obstructing all efforts to remove it. Although highly illegal the income from Alide providing a service was a 'life-saver'.
The authorities eventually allowed the family to move to Aliide's parents' house in Markuse although the land was off limits. Lonni and and Manni were born in the Markuse farm house.

In Ingria all hope that life would improve for the Finnish and Estonian diaspora were dashed in 1938 when Stalin gained supreme power. His paranoia sanctioned Yezhov's NKVD to further purge the country of potential enemies. Albert and Salme attended a Finnish school but the language was banned in 1938 effecting an end to their formal education. Months later, to extend the fields the family home was demolished. The family of nine slept in a barn until a family of Finnish stock welcomed them to their small house. Thankfully, the Georgijevs found a larger home before the Winter of 1938 and in Spring 1941 moved 30km north to Vitino. There were air-fields around Vitino which provided employment for Albert and Meeta but as a war with Germany progressed these were targeted by the German Luftwaffe.

When the bombing became intense the family (plus horse and cow) hid in dense woodland. They dug a ditch into the side of a railway embankment with added cover. Here they were safe from the shells falling on Vitino but Russian soldiers discovered them and ordered them to move, but move where?

Aliide ideally would like to have started for Estonia (90km) but Vassili still had contacts in Leningrad (ex St Petersburg), less than 50km away.

En-route to Leningrad they ran into many travellers fleeing Leningrad including an old friend of Vassili. Vassili was warned not to continue. The German bombardment of the city was said to be unrelenting. As the airfields around Vitino were still being targeted the family returned to the forest hideout. Here they lived for two weeks.

On one unforgettable morning Albert recalled clearly how he and his sisters were woken by his mother and told to stand in a clearing outside the hideaway. In the very distance up high on the embankment stood a line of soldiers in silhouette. Mindful that they had returned to the site after being ordered to leave they were anxious about a hostile response. To their surprise the soldiers turned out to be German, moreover they were respectful and polite. Albert remembers his mother speaking in Estonian and happily making tea for the lead officer and Estii interpreter. This patrol made a lasting impression on Albert. The family were advised to get a signed travel pass from a given senior officer billeted nearby, and to stock up with supplies before heading for Narva. Despite searching for food in various villages little could be found. In some areas homes were left abandoned leaving a rather grand manor Romanov type house open to the Georgijevs. Albert and his father readied the horse and wagon for the long trip. One of the older daughters milked the cow and the family set off.

Travelling east from Estonia retreating Russian troops were a danger to the family heading in the opposite direction especially if their allegiance was questionable.
