Ukraine Invasion: A Sad Reminder

The invasion of Ukraine is an unwelcome reminder that my own father, Albert, and his family were subjected to similar ordeals imposed by a Russian dictatorship driven by paranoia.

The turmoil started with his grandparents being dispossessed of all they owned. For a child that was difficult to comprehend. However, nothing could prepare him or his family for the programme of genocide openly planned by the Russian State.

In blue stripes my father Albert Georgijev poses next to his mother Aliide in Niiskovitsa, Ingria. His father, Vassili is absent. Aliide has good reason to look anxious: Stalin's propaganda machine has begun to demonise an entire demographic encouraging a hatred of so-called kulaks. The fate of this family depended on the appointed politruk (political official) responsible locally for compiling the lists of 'enemies of the people,' or 'kulaks'.

1930 L-R: Ludmilla, Rosalie, Salme, Aliide, Niina and Albert.

Madis and Maarja Noole Aliide's parents, (front left) were successful farmers in Markuse: they provided regular employment and sold produce locally in Gatchina and St Petersburg. For these 'crimes' they were targeted. Condemned as Kulaks all they owned was confiscated and they were sentenced to be deported. However, this order was never expedited.

Aliide was one of four children brought up in the Markuse farm house, built by her father Madis. Aliide was a skilled seamstress. She had trained in Narva, Estonia, stayed with relatives and left vowing to return. She was the proud owner of a sewing machine which she used throughout her life to provide a service of tailoring and repairing cloth. Albert could recall the rhythm of the machine and the close attention of his mother when he was taught to stitch and darn.

Vassili Georgijev was an orphan raised by a Finnish widow in Niiskovitsa, Ingria. Details of his origin are scant and the Bulgarian surname a mystery. The wedding photograph mainly consists of Aliide's family.

After years of building up his farm Vassili lost everything to the 'Collectives'. He was put to work as a labourer on what was his own land, permitted a single cow (photo), and paid in grain which he had to take to the mill to be ground to flour. The humiliation queueing at the mill was further degrading. Despite obstacles he left the employ of the Collective to deliver goods with his horse and cart.

Everyday family life became increasingly difficult for Aliide. Part of the family home was reappropriated as a Collective shop which sold farm produce and lots of vodka. Public access to the house compromised the safety of Aliide and the older daughters.
Aliide also had to endure spates of drunkenness from a despairing Vassili.

When an attempt was made to remove the sewing machine an altercation ensued with Aliide and her children obstructing all efforts to remove it. Although such enterprise was highly illegal the income generated was critical.
Eventually the family were allowed to move to the empty house in Markuse. In the peace that followed Lonni and Manni were born.

1930s INGRIA (Markuse, unmarked, is nr Gatchina)

Madis and Maarja were not exiled to the East; when local agents came to collect them they could not be located and missed the train at nearby Gatchina. For some reason there were no consequences and they were relocated in nearby Volosova. Despite warnings Madis and Maarja made clandestine visits to their offspring in the area.

The genocide of Finnish-Estonian Ingrians intensified when Stalin gained supreme power in 1938. New orders from Stalin supported the NKVD to further purge the country of potential enemies. To attack the Ingrian culture at its roots all instruction from 1938 had to be delivered in Russian only. The education of Albert and Salme effectively ended as they attended a Finnish speaking Ingrian school.

Another State Order extended the fields of Collectivised farms. The Markuse house was isolated. The family were duly evicted and the home demolished. The Georgijevs were made homeless.

They slept in a barn until a family of Finnish stock took them in. Floor space here was so limited that the adults had to sleep in the hall and landing. Thankfully, the Georgijevs found a larger home before the Winter of 1938 and in Spring 1941 moved 30km north to Vitino. There were air-fields around Vitino which provided employment for Albert and Meeta but as the war with Germany progressed these were targeted by the Luftwaffe.

When the bombing became more intense the family (plus horse and cow) left their Vitino home for the safety of woodland. They dug a ditch into the side of an embankment and added cover. Here they felt safer from the shells falling on Vitino but Russian soldiers soon discovered them and ordered them to move out of the woods. Vitino, it was feared, was not safe. An alternative was needed. It is likely that at this point that Aliide would have opted to go west to Estonia

Vassili still had contacts in Leningrad (50km east), but en-route they were stopped by people fleeing from the horrors of Leningrad. Given the problems around Vitino the family returned to the forest hideout. Here they managed to live undisturbed for two weeks away from the front.

A Surreal Experience

Albert recalled a morning in hiding that stayed fixed in memory. He and his sisters had been woken early. They were being watched. Silhouetted and silent, figures stood still atop the adjacent embankment. Anxious about being caught again they were relieved that the soldiers were German with a contingent of Estonians. Albert remembers his mother happily making tea for them.

By mid-March 1942 the area was occupied by German troops. Vassili secured a travel permit for the trek to Narva but he was not able to add to provisions. In an unearthly setting many homes and shops across the area were empty or damaged or both. Standing aloft was a grand Romanov-type manor. Here the two parents and seven children decamped.

Early on the morning of the 22nd March 1942 Albert and Vassili readied the horse and wagon for the trip to Narva. They spent four days and three nights on the road sleeping on the road side when night fell totally exhausted. Advancing German units had presented no problems. Indeed, they may well have helped the stream of refugees leaving Ingria united in their grief of loss of identity and hatred of Stalin.

The Georgijev family reached the border town of Narva on the 25th March 1942. They were immediately quarantined, bathed and fed. Vassili and Aliide had managed to get the children to safety. They were all thoroughly exhausted and weak. With sleep and nutrition all would physically recover from the ordeal. All except one. Aliide died just days later of typhus fever. She did not have the strength to fight the illness.
